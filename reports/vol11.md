## INSIGHTS

**INSIGHT 1 — The AMMC Correspondent Bank of Record Is the First African Solution to the Banking Access Problem**
*Confidence: HIGH*

Every African crypto regulatory framework to date has faced the same structural failure mode: operators receive licences but cannot access banking rails because no bank will take on the AML/CFT risk of being a crypto correspondent. AMMC's draft licensing conditions solve this architecturally. The Correspondent Bank of Record mechanism requires each DASP applicant to designate a single primary Moroccan banking correspondent before licence issuance, with that bank accepting KYC relay, reporting, and fiat settlement obligations as a licensing condition, not a post-licence negotiation. The result: the first operator-bank pair to clear AMMC review locks in both an operating licence and the bank's dominant settlement position.

This mechanism will replicate. Ghana's SEC, Rwanda's CMA, and Tanzania's BOT have all cited the Morocco framework in working-group minutes within the past 90 days. When AMMC issues the first full licence under these conditions, it becomes a template. The banking access problem — which has killed more African crypto frameworks in implementation than hostile regulation — now has a tested African-origin solution.

**INSIGHT 2 — M-Pesa API Schema Signal Is Builder Behaviour**
*Confidence: HIGH*

The `digital_asset` object class introduced in the M-Pesa developer sandbox this week carries four fields: `asset_type`, `wallet_address`, `stablecoin_symbol`, `custody_provider`. These fields do not exist speculatively — they map exactly to CBK payment provider tier compliance requirements as gazetted June 1. Safaricom built this after the gazette was published. Infrastructure built in response to regulatory specifics is the strongest signal type in the ACIOS classification framework — it represents committed engineering resource, not strategic positioning. The AGM is June 15. The API schema was committed June 7. The window is five days.

**INSIGHT 3 — BCEAO Civil Society Gap Is a Legitimacy Risk Being Ignored by Commercial Respondents**
*Confidence: MEDIUM*

All four confirmed BCEAO consultation respondents are commercial operators or institutional players. The BCEAO's draft framework is being reviewed without a single financial inclusion, remittance corridor, or civil society submission in the record. BCEAO's stated mandate includes rural financial access. A framework adopted without civil society engagement is vulnerable to post-adoption challenge on legitimacy grounds — exactly the pattern that stalled the CEMAC framework in 2023. Any operator, DFI, or NGO filing a financial-inclusion-framed response before June 25 immunises themselves against this vulnerability and differentiates their submission from four commercially identical filings. The BCEAO will notice the gap in the record. Filing into it is not altruism — it is competitive positioning.

**INSIGHT 4 — Nigeria Day 47 Plus RFC Format Signal Is Strategic Silence Approaching Tipping Point**
*Confidence: MEDIUM*

The NGN premium floor has held for 47 consecutive days — the longest uninterrupted floor in the ACIOS dataset. The CBN has not issued a public statement on digital assets since Q1. A second Tier 3 source has now described the RFC format as "request for information and comments" — a softer instrument than a proposed framework, designed to gather market intelligence before commitment. The convergence of 47-day floor consolidation, RFC format signal, and second source corroboration suggests Nigeria is not hostile to engagement — it is characteristically late to announce what it has already decided internally. The tipping point between strategic silence and formal engagement is approaching.

Yellow Card filed all three CBK tiers simultaneously (vol10). Yellow Card submitted to BCEAO consultation (vol10). Yellow Card filed the SECZ mapping response this week.
